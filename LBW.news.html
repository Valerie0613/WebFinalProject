Life Below Water


Study doubles and decimates prior
estimated lifespans for whale species

Publish on 21/12/2024. By. Carley Rosengreen

Dr Peter Corkeron in the field.

New research co-authored by a Griffith University researcher into the lifespans of baleen whales has yielded boom-or-bust results for two well-known species, with one species‚Äô age estimates almost doubling from what was previously thought and the other drastically reduced due to human-driven impacts.

Key Points:

Background and Findings

  1. Research Objective:
    • Reassess lifespan estimates for Southern Right Whales (SRW) and North Atlantic Right Whales (NARW).
    • Address the underestimation caused by industrial whaling.

  2. Lifespan Estimates:
    • Southern Right Whales: Median lifespan: 73.4 years, with 10% living beyond 131.8 years.
    • North Atlantic Right Whales: Median lifespan: 22.3 years, with 10% living beyond 47.2 years.

Impact of Industrial Whaling

  1. Reason for Lifespan Underestimation
    • Whaling eliminated older whales, skewing lifespan data.
    • Historical reluctance to acknowledge whales' extreme longevity.

  2. Effects on North Atlantic Right Whales
    • Lifespan drastically reduced due to human factors (e.g., fishing gear entanglements, vessel collisions).
    • Extended calving intervals and delayed reproduction hinder population recovery.

Scientific and Conservation Implications

  1. Insights into Whale Biology
    • Whales can live over 150 years, challenging traditional assumptions.
    • Extreme longevity might be common among baleen whale species.

  2. Conservation Challenges
    • Misunderstanding biological traits complicates conservation efforts.
    • Highlighting the long-term impact of whaling on NARW populations.
An international research team has revised the lifespan estimates for southern right whales (SRW) and North Atlantic right whales (NARW) , revealing they live longer than previously thought. This correction follows centuries of whaling, which disproportionately targeted older whales, distorting lifespan data.

The study, led by Dr. Greg Breed from the University of Alaska Fairbanks, along with Dr. Els Vermeulen and Dr. Peter Corkeron, analyzed 40+-year mark-recapture databases. Their findings show that the median lifespan of SRWs is 73.4 years, with 10% of individuals living beyond 131.8 years.

Dr Peter Corkeron in the field.

NARW lifespans have been significantly shortened due to human impacts, with a median lifespan of 22.3 years and only 10% of individuals living past 47.2 years.
Researchers highlighted the unrecognized potential for great longevity among balaenid whales and possibly other great whales, a fact previously masked by the demographic disruptions caused by industrial whaling.
Dr. Peter Corkeron stressed the biological and conservation implications of this finding. While it was once believed that right whales lived only into their 70s ,the study now estimates that 10% can live past 130 years, with some possibly exceeding 150 years. This discovery underscores how historical whaling distorted lifespan data, emphasizing the need for improved conservation efforts.

  • Improved Age Modelling:
    New methods for modelling whale ages combined with recovering populations after industrial whaling have provided better lifespan estimates.

  • Female North Atlantic Right Whales (NARW):
    Recent models show that few female NARW survive past 50 years, with a median lifespan in the early 20s, significantly shorter than southern right whales.

  • Human-Caused Mortality:
    The shortened lifespan is largely due to entanglements in fishing gear and vessel collisions.

  • Reproductive Decline:
    Historically, NARW females started calving at 9-10 years with a calf every 3-4 years. Now, most begin calving in their mid-teens with an extended 7-year calving interval.

  • Conservation Challenges:
    Human-driven impacts have led to reduced calf production, making population recovery challenging.

  • Link Between Whaling and Current Decline:
    Industrial whaling distorted lifespan data, making it harder to manage the current conservation crisis effectively.

  • Research Collaboration:
    The study was conducted by the University of Alaska Fairbanks, University of Pretoria, and Griffith University.

  • Publication:
    Findings were published in Science Advances, emphasizing the unrecognized longevity potential in Balaenid whales.
